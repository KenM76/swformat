# SWFormat — Legal Posture

> Plain-English statement of the project's legal basis. Not legal
> advice. SWFormat is a community-maintained, independent open-source
> project; legal/IP questions go via the project's public repository
> (open an issue).

---

## 1. What this project is

SWFormat is an open-source library that reads, modifies, and (when
complete) writes files in SOLIDWORKS's native on-disk formats
(`.sldprt`, `.sldasm`, `.slddrw`) using only:

- Documentation generated by reverse-engineering legitimately-obtained
  files
- Open-source prior art (notably `schwitters/openswx`, MIT-licensed)
- Standard Python libraries (zlib, struct, xml.etree)

The project DOES NOT:

1. Decrypt encrypted SOLIDWORKS files (3DEXPERIENCE-vault files are
   detected and reported as unsupported; no attempt is made to bypass
   their encryption).
2. Circumvent any technical protection measure.
3. Replicate or replace the SOLIDWORKS application itself.
4. Include, redistribute, or derive from SOLIDWORKS source code or
   any SOLIDWORKS-proprietary binary.

## 2. Legal basis: interoperability reverse engineering

SWFormat operates under the interoperability reverse-engineering
exemptions present in copyright law worldwide:

### United States

**17 U.S.C. § 1201(f)**:

> Notwithstanding the provisions of subsection (a)(1)(A), a person who
> has lawfully obtained the right to use a copy of a computer program
> may circumvent a technological measure that effectively controls
> access to a particular portion of that program for the sole purpose
> of identifying and analyzing those elements of the program that are
> necessary to achieve interoperability of an independently created
> computer program with other programs, and that have not previously
> been readily available to the person engaging in the circumvention,
> to the extent any such acts of identification and analysis do not
> constitute infringement under this title.

This statutory exemption explicitly authorizes reverse engineering of
file format encodings when the purpose is interoperability with
"independently created computer programs."

### European Union

**Directive 2009/24/EC, Article 6 (Decompilation)**:

> The authorisation of the rightholder shall not be required where
> reproduction of the code and translation of its form within the
> meaning of points (a) and (b) of Article 4(1) are indispensable to
> obtain the information necessary to achieve the interoperability of
> an independently created computer program with other programs,
> provided that the following conditions are met:
> (a) those acts are performed by the licensee or by another person
>     having a right to use a copy of a program, or on their behalf by
>     a person authorised to do so;
> (b) the information necessary to achieve interoperability has not
>     previously been readily available to the persons referred to in
>     point (a); and
> (c) those acts are confined to the parts of the original program
>     which are necessary in order to achieve interoperability.

### Other jurisdictions

Most jurisdictions with developed copyright law have analogous
interoperability exemptions (UK CDPA 1988 §50B, Canada Copyright Act
§30.61, Australian Copyright Act §47D, etc.). SWFormat is intended to
operate within these exemptions everywhere.

## 3. User expectations

SWFormat is intended for users who:

1. **Have legitimate access to the files they process.** SWFormat does
   not facilitate access to files the user has no right to read.
2. **Use SWFormat for interoperability purposes** — reading, editing,
   or analyzing their own SW files using independently-created tools.
3. **Have a current SOLIDWORKS license** (typically — the most common
   use case is "my company has SW licenses; I want to bulk-edit my
   own work files faster than the COM API allows").

If you are a SOLIDWORKS user without a license who somehow obtained
files, SWFormat may help you read them — but you almost certainly
have other problems to address first.

## 4. Relationship to the SOLIDWORKS EULA

The SOLIDWORKS EULA, like most commercial software EULAs, contains a
"no reverse engineering" clause. The interoperability exemptions
described above are STATUTORY exemptions to copyright that override
contract-based "no RE" clauses in jurisdictions where such overrides
are recognized (this includes the US, EU, and most developed legal
systems).

The position is:

- SWFormat does not violate copyright (statutory interop exemption)
- The "no RE" clause in the EULA, to the extent it purports to prohibit
  what the statute authorizes, is unenforceable in those jurisdictions
- This is a well-established legal precedent (e.g., *Sega v. Accolade*,
  977 F.2d 1510 (9th Cir. 1992); *Sony v. Connectix*, 203 F.3d 596
  (9th Cir. 2000))

Users in jurisdictions that do NOT recognize statutory interop
exemptions should evaluate their local law before using SWFormat.

## 5. Distribution and contribution

SWFormat is distributed under Apache-2.0. Contributors retain copyright
of their contributions and grant the standard Apache-2.0 inbound license.

**Contributions MUST NOT**:

- Include SOLIDWORKS-proprietary source code or binaries
- Include client / employer work-product files unless the contributor
  has explicit permission
- Include files acquired through unauthorized means

**Reference prior art MUST**:

- Be license-compatible with Apache-2.0 (MIT and BSD are compatible;
  GPL and AGPL are not)
- Be attributed in `research/<source>_notes/README.md`

The corpus IP rules (`test/corpus/`) are enforced in CI:

- Only files marked `license_status: synthetic` in `corpus.config.json`
  may be checked into the repo
- `owner` files are referenced by absolute path; never copied in-tree
- A CI check fails the build if any `.sld*` file is added under
  `test/corpus/` without a corresponding `synthetic` entry in
  `corpus.config.json`

## 6. Patent posture

SWFormat takes no position on patents. CAD format encodings are
generally not patent-protected (formats themselves are not patentable
subject matter under most patent law), but specific algorithms used
inside (e.g., a particular spline encoding, a specific compression
variant) MIGHT be. Users assume their own patent-infringement risk
when using SWFormat to process or produce files.
